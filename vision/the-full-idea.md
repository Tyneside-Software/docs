# The full idea behind Tyneside Software

I’m building something bigger than just another software company. It’s a **three-pronged plan** that combines serious tech, real local business support, and genuine social impact — all rooted in the North East and designed to create lasting prosperity here.

## 1. The Software Company (Tyneside Software)

I’m creating vertical logistics and work-management software for any business that sends people out to jobs — scheduling, routing, customer portals, invoicing, the works. It’s the kind of tool that saves hours every day and makes field-service businesses run like clockwork.

We’re starting with cleaning companies because that’s where I have immediate access and proof-of-concept. The first version will be completely free for local cleaners (starting with Joanne, who I already work with). No catch — it’s an investment in reputation and real-world testing.

## 2. Supporting Local Entrepreneurs (the cleaning side)

At the same time I’m helping Joanne grow her new cleaning business. Free software, a proper website I’m building for her, and paid work through the charity angle. She gets everything she needs to scale, and I get a real customer who’s already bought in. It’s win-win and gives me a living, breathing case study from day one.

## 3. The Charity (social impact + publicity engine)

I’m personally funding a charity that gives free deep-clean “welcome home” packages to every new parent in the North East (starting in our local postcode). Cleaners get paid work, new parents get practical help when they need it most, and the whole thing demonstrates the software in action while doing real good.

## Why I’m doing this (the bigger picture)

I grew up in Seaham watching the old industries disappear and seeing too much reliance on government subsidies. I’ve spent my career scaling software companies (CloudTrade exit at £25.8m, Advanced, now Cloud Connect at £600k ARR and growing fast), but I’m restless. I want to build something that creates real jobs and real capability in Tyneside — not just for me, but for the next generation.

This is my long-term play: my son Lewis starts part-time coding at 13 (he turns 13 next March), and my daughters will get the same opportunity. In seven years he’ll have seven years of professional experience in a public company. The goal is generational wealth, generational capability, and a “jobs engine” for the region. Trickle-down economics done right — by actually building the capability locally.
